# Narrative Frameworks

Deep reference for structuring presentation narratives. Load this when building persuasion-heavy, pitch, or keynote outlines.

---

## The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto)

The governing logic for all business presentations:

1. **Start with the answer** — State your conclusion or recommendation first
2. **Group supporting arguments** — Organize evidence into 2–4 logical groups
3. **Order logically within groups** — Time order, structural order, or importance order
4. **Support with evidence** — Each argument backed by data, examples, or logic

**Application to slides:**
- Slide 1–2: State the recommendation/conclusion
- Slides 3–N: Each group of supporting arguments gets a section
- Final slides: Reinforce the conclusion and state the ask

**Anti-pattern:** Building up slowly to a "reveal" at the end. Executives stop listening after slide 3 if they don't know where you're going.

## Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR)

The McKinsey storytelling backbone:

| Element | Purpose | Typical slides |
|---|---|---|
| **Situation** | Establish shared context — what everyone agrees on | 1–2 slides |
| **Complication** | Introduce the tension — why the status quo won't work | 2–3 slides |
| **Resolution** | Present your answer with evidence | 4–8 slides |

**Why it works:** The complication creates urgency. Without it, the audience has no reason to care about your resolution.

**Variations:**
- **S-C-R-A** (add Ask): End with a specific request or decision needed
- **S-C-R-I** (add Implications): End with "what happens if we don't act"
- **Hook-S-C-R**: Open with a provocative statement before the situation

## Duarte Sparkline (Nancy Duarte)

Structure a presentation as alternation between "what is" and "what could be":

```
What Is → What Could Be → What Is → What Could Be → ... → New Bliss
```

**How it works:**
- **What Is**: Current reality, problems, pain points
- **What Could Be**: The envisioned future, the solution, the possibility
- **New Bliss**: The transformed state once the audience acts

The contrast between "what is" and "what could be" creates emotional tension that keeps the audience engaged.

**Best for:** Keynotes, vision presentations, change management, product launches where you're selling a future state.

**Application to slides:**
- Alternate between problem/tension slides and vision/possibility slides
- Build the contrast progressively — each "what could be" is more compelling
- End in "new bliss" — the audience sees the full picture of the transformed future

## The Hero's Journey (Adapted for Presentations)

For narrative-heavy talks where the audience IS the hero:

1. **Ordinary World** — The audience's current state
2. **Call to Adventure** — The opportunity or challenge presented
3. **Refusal / Obstacles** — Why it's hard, why others fail
4. **Mentor / Guide** — You (the presenter) offer the path
5. **Transformation** — What changes when they follow the path
6. **Return with Elixir** — The new capabilities/results they'll have

**Key insight:** The presenter is NOT the hero. The audience is. The presenter is the guide (Yoda, not Luke).

## Pitch Deck Narrative (Investor Presentations)

The canonical 10–12 slide structure:

| # | Slide | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Title + One-liner | Instant clarity on what you do |
| 2 | Problem | Pain point with evidence it's real and large |
| 3 | Solution | Your answer — clear, specific, differentiated |
| 4 | Demo / How it works | Show, don't tell — product in action |
| 5 | Traction | Proof it works — metrics, growth, customers |
| 6 | Market | Size and dynamics — TAM/SAM/SOM or similar |
| 7 | Business model | How you make money — clear unit economics |
| 8 | Competition | Your positioning — why you win |
| 9 | Team | Why THIS team will execute |
| 10 | Ask | What you need and what you'll do with it |

**Rules for pitch decks:**
- Hook in the first 30 seconds or lose the room
- Traction > promises. Show numbers, not just vision.
- The "Problem" slide must make investors FEEL the pain
- Competition slides that show "we're better at everything" are not credible — show your specific wedge
- End with a concrete ask, not "let's discuss"

## Amazon 6-Pager Principles (Applied to Slides)

Amazon's narrative document philosophy, translated to presentation structure:

1. **No bullet points in the narrative** — Tell a complete story with full sentences (adapt: slides use bullets, but speaker notes tell the story)
2. **Start with the customer** — Frame everything from the end-user perspective
3. **Work backwards** — Start with the desired outcome, then explain how to get there
4. **Data over anecdote** — Support every claim with a specific metric
5. **Disagree and commit** — Present trade-offs honestly, then commit to a recommendation

**Application:** Use these as quality checks after generating an outline. Does each slide serve the customer narrative? Is every claim backed by data? Are trade-offs acknowledged?

## Choosing the Right Framework

| Presentation type | Primary framework | Secondary |
|---|---|---|
| Executive recommendation | Pyramid + SCR | — |
| Investor pitch | Pitch Structure | SCR for problem framing |
| Product launch / keynote | Duarte Sparkline | Hero's Journey |
| Change management | SCR + Sparkline | — |
| Technical proposal | Pyramid | SCR for motivation |
| Conference talk | Hero's Journey | Sparkline |
| Sales presentation | SCR | Pitch Structure |
| Internal strategy | Pyramid | — |

## Narrative Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any outline's narrative:

- [ ] Can you state the presentation's core message in one sentence?
- [ ] Does the opening create urgency or curiosity within 30 seconds?
- [ ] Is there clear tension/conflict that the presentation resolves?
- [ ] Does every slide advance the argument? (Remove any that don't)
- [ ] Does the closing provide forward momentum?
- [ ] Would the narrative still make sense if you removed all data? (Story structure is independent of evidence)
- [ ] Is the audience the hero, not the presenter?
